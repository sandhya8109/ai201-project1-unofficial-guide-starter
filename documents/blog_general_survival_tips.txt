Find somewhere where you can get good, cheap take-away food.
If you’re living in self-catered accommodation (or even catered accommodation), there are going to be days when you don’t want to cook. Finding good, cheap food can definitely cheer you up at the end of a shitty week.
I personally frequented my favourite fish and chip shop, and a great little falafel place. (James Finn will confirm.)
Take downtime.
Going to university is damn busy. Even the most extroverted extrovert will need time alone. You’re in an entirely new place with entirely new people, taking time to yourself is hugely important for your well-being.
I spend a lot of time visiting places in my new city, but also doing my usual reading and writing.
Find out where you can get help.
Problems with loneliness and mental health are extremely prevalent at university, and your university should have options available to get help should you need it. You never know when you might need help, and don’t think that just because you’ve never been depressed (for example) means that you never will.
Stay in touch with your parents (assuming you’re on good terms).
I promise you’ll appreciate it just as much as they do. There is this idea that once you go to university, you should only see them every three months and contact should be minimal. Don’t do this.
Have fun.
Do whatever you do to have fun. Meet people. Learn. Study hard.
Drink responsibly.
Get plenty of sleep.
You will need it!
